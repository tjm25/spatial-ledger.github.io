Spatial Ledger Analysis: Crawley Borough Local Plan 2023-2040

This Local Plan acknowledges Crawley's significant housing need but deliberately plans to meet only 42% of it within the borough, citing constraints while offloading the majority (58%) onto neighbouring areas via the Duty to Cooperate. While transparent about the shortfall, restrictive policies on density and character, coupled with extensive land safeguarding for the airport, risk hindering even this low target.
Target vs Need: FAILS (Plans for 42% of Need)
Spatial Strategy: CONSTRAINED (Central focus, exports need)
Policy Blockers: MODERATE RISK (Discretionary policies)
Transparency: GOOD (Acknowledges shortfall)

Housing Target & Delivery: Planning for Failure?

Crawley faces a significant housing shortage. The government's Standard Method indicates a need for 755 new homes per year (12,835 over the 17-year plan period). However, the Local Plan sets a drastically lower minimum target of just 5,330 homes (average 314 per year), explicitly stating this is a 'supply-led' figure dictated by constraints, not need.

This means the plan actively aims to leave 7,505 homes (58% of the total need) unbuilt within Crawley, relying heavily on neighbouring authorities to pick up the slack. While past delivery sometimes exceeded previous lower targets, the plan's own trajectory shows delivery dropping sharply after 10 years, indicating limited long-term ambition within the borough boundary.

Evidence: Policy H1 (p170), Para 12.8 (p163), Figure 22 (p164), Para 12.36 (p171), Para 12.39 (p171), Housing Trajectory (p331).

Spatial Strategy: Central Focus, Peripheral Problems

The plan focuses new housing on completing the Forge Wood neighbourhood, redeveloping sites within the Town Centre, and identifying some key housing sites. Higher densities are encouraged in central, accessible locations like the Town Centre and near transport hubs.

However, this central focus is significantly undermined by major constraints. Large swathes of land north of the borough are safeguarded for potential Gatwick Airport expansion (Policy GAT2), effectively sterilising them for housing. Development outside the defined Built-Up Area is heavily restricted (Policy CL8), and the High Weald National Landscape adds further constraints to the south (Policy CL9). The primary strategy for meeting the majority of Crawley's housing need involves pushing it outside the borough entirely (Paras 12.17-12.23).

Evidence: Policy H2 (p173), Policy TC3 (p155), Policy CL3/CL4 (p51/53), Policy GAT2 (p144), Policy CL8 (p62), Policy CL9 (p65), Paras 12.17-12.23 (p165-167).

Hidden Constraints & Policy Blockers: The 'Character' Catch?

While promoting higher densities in principle (Policy CL4), the plan includes significant caveats. Minimum densities only apply "...unless the existing character justifies a lower figure." This creates a major loophole allowing lower-density schemes even in sustainable locations if subjective 'character' arguments prevail.

Numerous policies emphasise protecting heritage (HA1-HA7), landscape (CL6, CL7), Green Infrastructure (GI1), and amenity (DD1). Wording like "respect," "enhance," "preserve," and "unacceptable impact" is common, providing planners with considerable discretion to restrict or refuse development, potentially undermining even the allocated sites and windfall assumptions.

Policy CL4 Loophole: "[A] minimum density of at least 45 dwellings per hectare will apply... In the following specific locations... major proposals must achieve the following minimum densities... unless the existing character justifies a lower figure..." (p53)
Evidence: Policy CL1-CL9 (p46-65), Policy DD1 (p68), Policy HA1-HA7 (p82-92), Policy GI1 (p207), Policy CL4 (p53).

Contradictions & Trade-offs: Growth vs Constraints

The plan's central contradiction is acknowledging a high housing need (755 dpa) while planning for drastically less delivery (avg 314 dpa). It promotes economic growth (Chapter 9) but severely restricts the housing needed to support that growth and workforce.

Environmental constraints like Water Neutrality (Policy SDC4) are presented as significant hurdles requiring complex offsetting, adding cost and delay. Airport safeguarding (Policy GAT2) is treated as a near-absolute block on development in the north. While the plan is transparent about these constraints and the resulting unmet need, the decision to prioritise these constraints so heavily over meeting housing need within the borough represents a significant trade-off, effectively exporting the housing challenge.

Acknowledging the Shortfall: "The constrained nature of Crawley’s land supply means that approximately... 58% of the borough’s predicted housing need... will need to be considered through Duty to Cooperate discussions..." (Para 12.39, p171)
Evidence: Policy H1 (p170), Policy SDC4 (p233), Policy GAT2 (p144), Para 12.12 (p164), Para 12.39 (p171).

Site Allocation & Trajectory: Front-Loaded & Uncertain

The plan relies on key sites like Forge Wood (nearing completion) and complex Town Centre opportunities (Policy H2, TC3). The housing trajectory (p331) shows that planned delivery is heavily front-loaded in the first 10 years, dropping sharply thereafter. This suggests limited confidence in long-term supply within the borough.

A significant portion (1,600 homes, or 100 dpa) of the planned supply relies on windfall sites (Para 12.16) – sites not yet identified. This accounts for nearly 30% of the planned delivery, adding a layer of uncertainty. Given the restrictive policies, even windfall delivery could be challenging.

Evidence: Policy H2 (p173), Policy TC3 (p155), Housing Trajectory (p331), Paras 12.14-12.16 (p165).

Policy Language & Implementation Risk: Room for Restriction

The use of discretionary language ("respect character", "appropriate scale", "unacceptable impact") throughout the plan creates implementation risk. While providing flexibility, these terms can also be used by decision-makers to resist development, even on allocated sites, if local opposition arises or subjective interpretations prevail.

The significant reliance on the Duty to Cooperate to meet the majority of Crawley's need carries inherent risks. Neighbouring authorities face their own constraints and housing pressures (Para 2.26, Fig 4), and there is no guarantee they will accept or deliver the 7,505 homes Crawley cannot accommodate. This creates significant uncertainty about whether Crawley's full housing need will ever be met.

Evidence: Discretionary wording in policies CL1, CL2, CL4, DD1, H3 etc. Paras 1.22-1.28 (p15), Para 2.26 (p26), Para 12.39 (p171).
