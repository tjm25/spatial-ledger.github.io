Merton Local Plan: Aspiring for Growth, Hindered by Constraints?

This Plan aims for significant housing but its lower-than-London-Plan average target, reliance on complex sites, and layers of potentially restrictive design/heritage policies create major risks to delivery.

Target vs Need: CAUTION
Spatial Strategy: CAUTION
Policy Blockers: HINDERING
Contradictions: CAUTION
Delivery Risk: HIGH

Housing Target: Falling Short of Ambition?

The Plan sets a target of delivering 12,084 homes between 2021/22 and 2037/38. While presented as meeting needs, this averages 755 homes per year – significantly less than the 918 homes/year target derived from the London Plan's 10-year requirement for Merton. Is this a realistic plan or an acceptance of under-delivery?

  • Plan Target: Minimum 12,084 homes 2021/22-2037/38 (average 755/year). Policy H11.2
  • London Plan Target Rate: 918 homes/year (based on 9,180 over 10 years 2019/20-2028/29). Para 11.2.3
  • Affordability Crisis: Plan acknowledges high house prices (£460k median) and affordability pressures. Para 11.1.3
  • Trajectory Risk: Housing trajectory shows a steep drop after the first year, with delivery heavily reliant on later phases and complex schemes. Figure 11.2.1
  • Affordable Housing: Strategic target of 50% affordable homes, but only 35% required on-site via Fast Track (lower than 50%), with viability arguments potentially reducing this further. Policy H11.1
"Merton’s housing target, which is set out in the London Plan 2021, is 9,180 homes for the ten-year period 2019/20 to 2028/29 or 918 homes per annum... Merton’s housing trajectory set out in Figure 11.2.1 is considered an appropriate approach for Merton... A stepped housing delivery target is appropriate" Paras 11.2.3 & 11.2.9
Why is a lower average target (755/year) over 16 years considered 'appropriate' when the identified need rate is 918/year?

Spatial Strategy: Growth Zones or Growth Constraints?

The plan correctly identifies the Opportunity Area (Wimbledon, South Wimbledon, Colliers Wood) and Morden Regeneration Zone as key growth locations. However, delivery hinges on these specific, complex areas coming forward, alongside a continued reliance on small sites which historically make up 50% of delivery but face viability challenges.

  • Key Growth Areas: Opportunity Area (5,000 homes target) and Morden Regeneration Zone (~2,000 homes target). Paras 1.2.44, 1.2.47, 5.1.13
  • Town Centre Focus: Prioritises intensification in town centres and near transport hubs. Policy EC13.1, Policy TC13.5
  • Small Sites Reliance: Acknowledges 85-95% of applications are small sites (under 10 units), contributing ~50% of past delivery. London Plan small sites target is 261 homes/year. Para 11.2.2, 11.2.3
  • Constraints: Opportunity Area delivery explicitly *not* predicated on Crossrail 2 (now unfunded). Para 1.2.47, 16.5.6

Policy Blockers: Designed for Delivery or Delay?

While aiming for high quality, the Plan introduces numerous potentially restrictive policies. Vague language around design, character, heritage, and tall buildings gives significant discretionary power to refuse development, even on allocated sites. This undermines the certainty needed to deliver homes at scale.

  • Design & Character: Policies require development to "respect", "respond positively to", "enhance" existing context, character, and local distinctiveness. Policies D12.1, D12.2, D12.3
  • Heritage Assets: Strong protection for designated and non-designated heritage assets and their settings, requiring clear justification for any harm. Policy D12.5
  • Tall Buildings: Strictly controlled, limited to specific locations and subject to detailed criteria including townscape analysis and Strategic Heights Diagrams. Policy D12.6, Maps p.389-391
  • Amenity: Frequent references to protecting amenity (noise, privacy, outlook, light) can be used subjectively to resist intensification. e.g., Policy D12.3(g)
  • Discretionary Language: Frequent use of terms like "appropriate", "sympathetic", "minimise harm", "unacceptable impact" creates uncertainty.
"Successful neighbourhoods are much more than just buildings. Historic environments, mature vegetation and existing communities all contribute to successful places, and as such must be considered when designing." Policy D12.1(1)
Necessary context, or a potential veto on growth dressed up as good design?

Contradictions: Green Goals vs Housing Need?

The Plan rightly promotes sustainability and protecting green spaces. However, there's a risk these laudable goals are used disproportionately to block needed housing, especially denser schemes in accessible locations. The strong protection for MOL and emphasis on biodiversity net gain, while positive in principle, adds layers of constraint that may conflict with meeting the housing target.

  • Climate Emergency vs Housing: Net-zero targets require sustainable design, potentially increasing costs and complexity, creating tension with housing viability/delivery speed. Chapter 2
  • Green Infrastructure: Strong protection for MOL, Open Space, SINCs, Green Corridors. Chapter 15
  • Biodiversity Net Gain: Requirement for 10% net gain adds another constraint/cost. Policy O15.3(d)
  • Trade-offs Unclear: Plan doesn't explicitly quantify how competing priorities (housing vs heritage vs MOL vs climate) will be weighed where they conflict.

Site Allocations: Viable Plots or Paper Parks?

The Plan allocates numerous sites, but many face significant constraints or long delivery timescales. Reliance on complex brownfield sites (former waste sites, industrial land), sites requiring relocations (health clinics, community centres), and those with heritage or flood risk issues raises questions about deliverability within the plan period.

  • Complex Sites: Examples include Mi1 (Benedict Wharf - former waste site, 10-15 yrs), Mi3 (Burn Bullock - heritage, pub restoration), Mi14 (United Westminster Schools - playing field, 5-10 yrs), Mi16 (Gasworks - contamination, 0-5 yrs), Mi18 (Wilson Hospital - healthcare relocation, 5-10 yrs).
  • Long Timescales: Many key sites listed with 5-10 or 10-15 year delivery horizons. Site Allocation chapters
  • Enabling Development: Some sites rely on 'enabling development' (e.g. Mo3 for sports hub), which can be complex to secure.
  • Indicative Capacities: Some sites have wide capacity ranges (e.g., Mi1 650-850 homes), suggesting uncertainty.

Delivery Trajectory: Realistic Pace or Wishful Thinking?

The housing trajectory relies on a very high delivery rate in the first year, followed by dips and later peaks dependent on major schemes. This backloading of delivery onto large, complex regeneration projects (Morden, Wimbledon Quarter, Estate Regen) represents a significant risk. If these schemes stall or under-deliver, the overall target will be missed.

  • Stepped Target: Starts high (918), drops to 450, rises to 1200, then falls to 548. Policy H11.2 Table
  • Backloading: Significant number of homes anticipated post-2029/30. Figure 11.2.1
  • Reliance on Major Schemes: Delivery heavily dependent on Morden Regeneration Zone (Mo1), Wimbledon Quarter (Wi16), and ongoing Estate Regeneration (High Path, Eastfields, Ravensbury). Para 11.2.9, 5.1.13
  • Monitoring Triggers: Plan review triggered if delivery falls >20% below trajectory over 3 years. Policy M17.1(2)
