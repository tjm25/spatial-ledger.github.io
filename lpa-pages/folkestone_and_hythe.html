Verdict: A Huge Bet on One Site

This Local Plan meets its calculated housing need on paper, a significant step up from its predecessor, but achieves this almost entirely by allocating a massive, long-term, and complex Garden Settlement (Otterpool Park), creating enormous delivery risk while potentially underutilising other less constrained locations.

Target vs Need: Meets OAN
Spatial Strategy: High Concentration Risk
Policy Blockers: Medium Risk
Contradiction Score: Low

Housing Target: Meeting the Numbers, Finally?

The Plan aims for 13,284 new homes between 2019/20 and 2036/37, averaging 738 homes per year.(Para 4.10, 4.42) This figure aligns with the Government's Standard Method calculation for Objectively Assessed Need (OAN) at the time the plan was prepared.(Para 4.9) This represents a significant increase from the previous 2013 Core Strategy target (around 400 homes/year planned),(Para 4.7) acknowledging the district's affordability pressures.

However, achieving this higher target relies heavily on a 'stepped' delivery approach, with lower numbers initially, ramping up significantly once the Garden Settlement starts delivering.(Para 4.46, Table 6.7) While the plan *identifies* enough land on paper (13,407 homes minimum),(Table 4.3) the actual delivery hinges precariously on the success of very large, complex sites, particularly the new Garden Settlement.

Target: 738 homes/year (Total 13,284)
Meets OAN (Standard Method): Yes
Constraint Impact on Target: Not explicitly lowered, but constraints heavily shape *location* of growth.

Spatial Strategy: All Eggs in the Otterpool Basket?

The strategy concentrates the vast majority of new strategic growth into the Otterpool Garden Settlement (policies SS6-SS9), located outside the Kent Downs AONB near the M20 and Westenhanger station.(Policy SS1) Other priorities include previously developed land in Folkestone (Seafront, Shorncliffe) and Hythe (west), plus regeneration in Romney Marsh.(Policy SS1)

This approach *does* avoid widespread development in the highly constrained AONB and high flood risk areas of Romney Marsh.(Para 4.14, Policy SS1) However, it places immense pressure on a single mega-project. The Growth Options Study justified this concentration,(Paras 4.157-4.169) but the risk of relying so heavily on one site for nearly half the plan's total housing (5,593 homes in plan period from Otterpool(Table 4.3)) is substantial. Are other, smaller, less complex opportunities being overlooked or underutilised?

Concentration Risk: The plan hinges on successfully delivering a new town from scratch. Failure or significant delays at Otterpool would likely cause the entire strategy to fail its housing targets.

Policy Blockers: Design Dreams vs Delivery Reality?

The Plan includes standard policies protecting heritage, landscape, and requiring high-quality design.(Policy SS3) For the Otterpool Garden Settlement, these standards are particularly high, demanding a "landscape-led" approach, "distinctive townscape", "beacon of best practice" for sustainability (low carbon, low waste, high water efficiency), and detailed design codes.(Policy SS6, SS7, SS8)

While laudable, such demanding and potentially subjective requirements ("respects", "sensitive", "appropriate") can introduce significant delays, costs, and uncertainty, potentially acting as blockers to delivery if applied inflexibly.(Para 4.90, 4.181, 4.182) The complexity of the multi-tiered approval process for Otterpool further adds to implementation risk.(Para 4.198)

Subjective Language Risk: High
Garden Settlement Policy Complexity: High
Potential for Delay: Medium-High

Contradictions & Trade-offs: Balancing Act or Illusion?

The Plan attempts to navigate the tension between high housing need and significant environmental constraints (AONB, coast, flood risk). It largely achieves this spatially, directing major growth *away* from the most sensitive areas towards the Otterpool site.(Policy SS1) Policies require mitigation for impacts (e.g., on AONB setting, flood risk, Natura 2000 sites) rather than using constraints as absolute vetoes.(Policy SS3, SS6, CSD4, CSD5)

The key trade-off – concentrating development impacts at Otterpool to protect other areas – seems relatively transparent, justified through the Growth Options Study.(Paras 4.153, 4.164) There are no glaring internal policy contradictions, but the fundamental challenge remains balancing the scale and pace of delivery required with the quality and sustainability ambitions, especially for Otterpool.

Site Delivery: Otterpool Gamble Looms Large

Existing major sites like Folkestone Seafront and Shorncliffe Garrison have permissions and are progressing.(Paras 4.148, 4.213, 4.226) However, the Plan's success rests overwhelmingly on the new allocation at Otterpool Park (targeting ~5,600 homes in the plan period, rising to 8,000-10,000 beyond).(Policy SS6) This is a decades-long project requiring massive infrastructure investment (including HS1 station upgrades, M20 junction work, new schools, health facilities) largely funded through developer contributions (S106, as CIL is disapplied).(Policy SS9, Para 4.209, Appendix 5)

The viability and deliverability of a project this large and complex, reliant on continuous market demand and intricate infrastructure phasing over 20+ years, represents a significant gamble. Smaller sites identified in the separate Places and Policies Local Plan and windfall contributions provide some buffer but cannot compensate if Otterpool falters.

Trajectory & Delivery: Backloaded and Hopeful

The official housing trajectory(Appendix 3, Table 6.7) shows a 'stepped' approach, meeting the overall target of 13,284 homes (delivering 13,407). However, delivery is heavily backloaded. The annual average required jumps from 622/year (2019-24) to 885/year (2024-29) before settling slightly lower. This peak relies entirely on Otterpool Park starting delivery strongly from around 2024/25 and ramping up quickly.

While not showing a *planned* shortfall, the trajectory appears optimistic about the build-out rate of a new settlement. Any delays in Otterpool's complex infrastructure delivery or a market downturn could easily derail these projections, leading to under-delivery against the target in later years.

Delivery Risk: The plan needs nearly 900 homes per year delivered mid-decade, heavily reliant on a brand new town hitting its stride immediately. This looks ambitious.
