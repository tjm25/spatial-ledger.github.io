Spatial Ledger Analysis: Bracknell Forest Local Plan (Adopted March 2024)

Overall Verdict

This Local Plan meets its minimum housing target on paper but relies heavily on complex brownfield sites, late-plan delivery, and potentially restrictive design policies, creating significant risks of underdelivery in practice. While positively focusing regeneration on Bracknell Town Centre, it shies away from challenging constraints like the Green Belt or setting genuinely ambitious targets needed to tackle affordability.

Target vs Need: Meets Minimum
Spatial Strategy: Mixed (Good PDL focus, some Greenfield)
Policy Blockers: High Risk (Vague design/character rules)
Contradictions: Moderate (Protectionism vs Delivery)

Housing Target & Delivery: Playing it Safe?

The Plan sets a minimum housing target based directly on the government's standard method calculation. While this meets the baseline requirement, it makes no explicit upward adjustment for Bracknell Forest's significant affordability pressures or potential unmet need from neighbouring areas. It relies on existing commitments, new allocations, and a significant windfall allowance to meet this number.

This approach avoids political difficulty but lacks the ambition needed to significantly improve housing affordability. Relying on nearly 1,200 windfall homes also introduces uncertainty into the supply pipeline.

Annual Target: 614 dwellings per annum (dpa). (Policy LP4)

Total Target (2020-2037): 10,438 net additional homes. (Policy LP4, Table 4)

Relation to Need: Target equals the calculated Local Housing Need (LHN) using the standard method. (Para 6.9, Table 3). No explicit mention of adjusting target upwards for affordability or unmet need from neighbours (unlike Hart taking Surrey Heath's). (Para 6.4-6.7)

Constraints Impact: While constraints (Green Belt, SPA) shape the *strategy*, the plan doesn't state these constraints were used to *reduce* the LHN figure itself. The target *is* the LHN. (Para 6.2, 5.5)

Land Allocation: Plan claims sufficient supply: 9,110 homes from commitments/windfalls + 2,080 homes from new allocations (net deliverable in plan period) = 11,190 total supply vs 10,438 target. (Tables 5, 6, 7, 8, Para 6.29). This includes a windfall allowance of 1,220 homes. (Table 5, Para 6.15)

Spatial Strategy: Town Centre Focus vs Greenfield Reality

The plan rightly prioritises development within and around Bracknell Town Centre, aiming to maximise Previously Developed Land (PDL) use and support regeneration. This is a positive step. However, significant housing delivery also relies on strategic greenfield sites on the edge of Bracknell (Beaufort Park) and Crowthorne (Derby Field).

While presented as balanced, this strategy reflects the pressure of constraints like the Green Belt (covering 35% of the borough) and the Thames Basin Heaths SPA, pushing some development onto less constrained, but less sustainable, greenfield locations. There's a risk that the easier-to-deliver greenfield sites progress while complex town centre PDL sites stall.

Priority Locations: Bracknell Town Centre regeneration (PDL focus), followed by Sandhurst & Crowthorne, then Binfield & North Ascot. (Policy LP1, LP2, Para 5.7-5.17)

Key Allocations:

  • Town Centre/Edge-of-Centre (PDL/Mixed): Peel Centre/The Point (BRA18 - 900 homes, 600 in plan), Eastern Gateway (BRA7 - 210 homes), Southern Gateway (BRA14/15/17 - 600 homes). (LP5, LP8, LP9, LP10)
  • Strategic Greenfield Extensions: Beaufort Park, Bracknell (BRA4 - 230 homes), Derby Field, Crowthorne (SAND5 - 220 homes). (LP5, LP6, LP7)
  • Smaller edge-of-settlement sites (mostly greenfield). (LP5, LP11-LP15)

Constraints: Green Belt, Thames Basin Heaths SPA, Flood Risk heavily influence where development *cannot* go. (Map 3, Para 5.5, 5.15, 5.20-5.21)

Hidden Constraints & Policy Blockers: The Devil's in the Detail

Despite allocating land, the plan contains numerous policies with vague, subjective wording that could hinder delivery. Policies demanding development "respects", "enhances", or is "sympathetic to" local character provide significant leverage for objectors and decision-makers to block or delay schemes, even on allocated sites.

Density is described as "flexible" but highest densities are sought only in the most central locations, potentially limiting capacity elsewhere. Green Belt policies use terms like "not materially larger" (replacement buildings) and "disproportionate additions" (extensions), defined by indicative percentage increases (20% / 40% volume) that can be very restrictive. The 'tall building' threshold outside the town centre is low (substantially taller than neighbours), potentially stifling gentle density increases.

Watch out for Weasel Words: Policies requiring development to "respond positively to" (LP36), "protect and enhance" (LP36), be "designed to a high standard that... makes a positive contribution to maintaining and enhancing local character" (LP3, G), or be "appropriate to the scale of the locality" (LP34) lack clear metrics and invite subjective blocking.

Design & Character: Policy LP28 ('Design Principles') and LP50 ('Design') require proposals to "respond to the physical, visual and functional context", "enhance and add quality", and "reflect and wherever possible enhance the identity of the area". Policy LP36 requires development outside settlements to "respond positively to... valued landscape features and characteristics". (LP3, LP28, LP36, LP50)

Density: Policy aims for efficient land use but density approach is "not to be overly prescriptive", guided by "context" and "character". Highest densities explicitly sought "in and around Bracknell Town Centre". (Para 5.34, 6.31)

Green Belt Restrictions: Replacement buildings generally "not materially larger" (indicative +20% volume limit). Extensions generally not "disproportionate additions" (indicative +40% volume limit). (Policy LP34, paras 9.88, 9.102)

Tall Buildings: Defined relatively low (5 storeys/15m in centre, or 'substantially taller' elsewhere). Require extensive justification including landscape/visual impact assessment against specific viewpoints. (Policy LP51, Appendix 6)

Contradictions & Trade-offs: Housing vs Heritage vs Hedges?

The plan presents numerous policy objectives (housing, economy, environment, heritage, design) but doesn't always clearly articulate how conflicts will be resolved. There's a risk that protectionist policies, particularly around heritage (LP29), landscape character (LP36), Green Belt (LP34), and biodiversity/trees (LP53, LP54), could be used to frustrate housing delivery on allocated or windfall sites.

For example, achieving high design quality and respecting character (LP28, LP50, LP36) could conflict with achieving efficient densities needed to meet housing targets, especially on constrained sites. The requirement for 10% Biodiversity Net Gain (LP53) and SPA mitigation (SANG - LP32) adds costs and complexity, particularly noted as potentially challenging viability on town centre sites (para 6.193). While necessary, these need careful balancing against housing delivery.

Potential Conflicts: Housing Need (LP4) vs Green Belt protection (LP34), Countryside protection (LP35), Landscape Character (LP36), Heritage (LP29), Biodiversity/Trees (LP53/54), Design Quality (LP28/50).

Green Belt Policy: Treats Green Belt purposes as paramount, requiring "very special circumstances" to justify inappropriate development. While standard policy, its strict application could limit options. (Policy LP34, Para 9.85)

Viability Challenges: Plan acknowledges viability may affect delivery of affordable housing tenure mix on town centre sites, suggesting potential conflict between policy goals and deliverability. (Para 6.193)

SPA Mitigation: SANG provision required for residential development near the SPA adds a significant requirement, potentially impacting site viability/layout, especially if required off-site. (Policy LP32)

Site-by-Site Assessment: Are Allocations Realistic?

The plan allocates several large, strategic sites alongside smaller ones. The town centre sites (Peel Centre, Eastern/Southern Gateways) offer excellent sustainable locations on PDL but are complex, potentially requiring significant remediation, infrastructure upgrades, and phasing, impacting deliverability within the plan period. The Peel Centre explicitly notes 300 homes are expected *beyond* 2037.

Strategic greenfield sites (Beaufort Park, Derby Field) appear more straightforward but still face constraints (SPA proximity, landscape buffers, access). Smaller sites contribute but won't deliver the bulk of the need. There's a risk the plan overestimates the speed and ease of delivery from the complex brownfield sites compared to the greenfield allocations.

Total Allocated (Deliverable in Plan): 2,080 homes. (Policy LP5, Para 6.29)

Key Site Details & Potential Issues:

  • Peel Centre (BRA18): 900 homes total, only 600 expected by 2037. Complex phasing, requires replacement supermarket, viability acknowledged as potentially challenging. Delivery starts late (est. 2031/32). (LP8, Para 6.96, 6.99, Delivery Section)
  • Eastern Gateway (BRA7): 210 homes. PDL in town centre. Delivery starts late (est. 2030/31). (LP9, Delivery Section)
  • Southern Gateway (BRA14/15/17): 600 homes. PDL in town centre, includes bus station relocation. Delivery starts late (est. 2030/31), long build out (7 years). (LP10, Para 6.163, Delivery Section)
  • Beaufort Park (BRA4): 230 homes. Greenfield extension. SPA mitigation (SANG required), flood risk, woodland loss. Delivery est. from 2028/29. (LP6, Delivery Section)
  • Derby Field (SAND5): 220 homes. Greenfield extension (playing fields). Requires replacement pitches, SPA mitigation, station parking. Delivery est. from 2027/28. (LP7, Delivery Section)

Density: Explicitly higher densities sought in town centre, lower on edge-of-settlement sites. (Para 6.31)

Trajectory & Delivery Expectations: Backloaded and Hopeful?

The plan includes a housing trajectory and claims a 5.33-year housing land supply at adoption (April 2023 calculation). While this figure appears healthy on the surface, it relies on significant delivery ramping up in the later years of the plan, particularly from the large, complex town centre sites (Peel Centre, Gateways) which aren't expected to start delivering homes until around 2030/31.

There's also a notable reliance on windfall sites (1,220 homes assumed). Any delays on the major allocated sites or underperformance on windfalls could quickly erode the land supply position. The backloading of delivery is a significant risk factor.

Claimed 5-Year Supply: 5.33 years (at 1st April 2023). (Appendix 1, Table 17)

Housing Trajectory (Figure 6 / Appendix 1 Table): Shows peaks in delivery expected in later years (e.g., 2028/29 onwards), coinciding with the projected start dates for major allocated sites.

Major Site Phasing: Beaufort Park (LP6) est. start 2028/29; Derby Field (LP7) est. start 2027/28; Peel Centre (LP8) est. start 2031/32 (part delivery post-plan); Eastern Gateway (LP9) est. start 2030/31; Southern Gateway (LP10) est. start 2030/31. (Delivery sections for LP6-LP10)

Windfall Reliance: 1,220 homes assumed from small and medium windfalls over the plan period. (Table 5)

Risk Alert: The trajectory shows over 4,000 homes projected from 2028/29 to 2036/37 from allocated SALP sites and new Local Plan sites combined (Appendix 1 Table). This heavy reliance on late-plan delivery, especially from complex sites, is a major vulnerability.

Policy Language & Implementation Risk: Certainty Optional?

Throughout the plan, but particularly in policies relating to design, character, Green Belt, and countryside development, the use of subjective and discretionary language persists. Phrases like "appropriate", "sympathetic", "where possible", "enhance", "respect character", and "not materially larger" create ambiguity.

This lack of clear, objective criteria risks inconsistent decision-making, empowers subjective opposition, and reduces certainty for applicants seeking to deliver much-needed housing, even on allocated sites. While some flexibility is needed, excessive discretion can become a tool for obstruction.

Examples of Vague Language:

  • LP3 (Sustainable Development Principles): Requires development to make a "positive contribution to maintaining and enhancing local character".
  • LP28 (Design Principles): Requires response to "physical, visual and functional context", aim to "enhance and add quality".
  • LP34 (Green Belt): Uses "materially larger" and "disproportionate additions" with indicative thresholds, leaving room for debate.
  • LP35 (Development in Countryside): Allows exceptions where "appropriate", requires minimizing impact on "character and appearance".
  • LP36 (Landscape Character): Requires proposals to "respond positively to valued landscape features".
  • LP50 (Design): Uses terms like "integrates well", "appropriate levels", "acceptable levels".

Certainty Impact: Lack of clear metrics makes it harder for developers to know if a scheme will be approved, increasing risk and potentially deterring investment or leading to lengthy appeals.
