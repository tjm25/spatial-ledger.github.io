Greater Norwich Local Plan: Housing Delivery Analysis

Spatial Ledger Plan Verdict

High Target, High Risk: The GNLP sets a housing goal above minimum requirements but risks under-delivery due to significant constraints (Nutrient Neutrality), potentially restrictive policies, and reliance on optimistic delivery rates.

While the plan allocates sufficient land and focuses growth logically, its success depends heavily on overcoming major hurdles and ensuring subjective policies don't become barriers to development.

Target vs Need: POSITIVE
Spatial Strategy: LARGELY POSITIVE
Policy Blockers: CAUTION
Implementation Risk: HIGH

Housing Target & Delivery: Ambition Meets Reality?

The plan is commendable for setting a target above the government's minimum calculation, acknowledging the need for more homes. However, historical delivery, combined with new and existing constraints like Nutrient Neutrality, raises questions about whether this ambitious target can realistically be met.

Annual Target (LHN Derived): 40,541 homes required 2018-2038 (approx. 2,027/year). (Strategy, Table 6)

Plan Target (Supply): Aims to deliver 45,041 homes 2018-2038 (approx. 2,252/year), providing an 11% buffer over LHN. (Strategy, Table 6, Policy 1)

Past Delivery: 8,728 homes built in first 4 years (2018-2022), averaging 2,182/year â€“ slightly above the LHN rate but below the plan's overall average target needed to hit 45k. (Strategy, Table 6)

Constraint Impact: Nutrient Neutrality explicitly cited as having constrained delivery since March 2022. (Strategy, para 53)

5-Year Land Supply: Claims 5.75 years supply at adoption (April 2023-March 2028). (Strategy, Appendix 4). This relies heavily on forecast delivery and assumes Nutrient Neutrality solutions are effective quickly.

Spatial Strategy: Sensible Focus, Sufficient Density?

The plan correctly directs most growth towards the Norwich urban area and its fringes (the 'Strategic Growth Area'), main towns, and transport corridors. This focus on sustainable locations is positive. The key question is whether density is being maximised appropriately in these prime locations.

Primary Focus: Around 70% of growth directed to the 'Strategic Growth Area'. (Strategy, para 171)

Hierarchy: Follows a clear hierarchy: Norwich Urban Area (62%), Main Towns (15%), Key Service Centres (8%), Village Clusters (9%). (Strategy, Table 7).

Brownfield Target: Aims for ~22% on brownfield land. (Strategy, para 166a). Is this sufficiently ambitious given the urban focus?

Strategic Sites: Major allocations cover logical large growth areas like East Norwich (STR.01), Anglia Square (STR.02), Airport Area (STR.03, STR.14), NRP (STR.05), Growth Triangle extensions (STR.10, STR.11).

Rural Growth: Appears controlled via Village Clusters (separate plan for South Norfolk) and limited windfall (Policy 7.5), aiming to prevent sprawl.

Focus on Sustainable Locations:Yes GOOD
Brownfield Ambition:Moderate OK

Policy Blockers: A Drag on Delivery?

The plan contains numerous policies requiring development proposals to meet criteria related to design, character, heritage, and landscape. While these aim for quality, the use of subjective language ('respect', 'enhance', 'appropriate') creates uncertainty and potential grounds for delay or refusal, even on allocated sites.

Subjective Design Criteria: Policy 2 (Sustainable Communities) demands 'beautiful, well-designed places', respecting 'local character'. Policy 3 requires designs to 'enhance the built and historic environment' and 'respect landscape character'. Site policies frequently repeat these potentially ambiguous requirements (e.g. S.BK.1, S.HA.1, S.TW.1).

Heritage Weighting: Policy 3 gives significant weight to conserving heritage assets and their settings. Site-specific policies often require Heritage Impact Assessments and protection of views or specific buildings (e.g. STR.01, STR.02, S.BK.1, S.DI.1).

Density vs Character Conflict: While Policy 2 sets minimum densities (40dph Norwich, 25dph elsewhere), achieving necessary higher densities in sustainable locations could potentially conflict with subjective interpretations of 'character' policies.

Process Hurdles: Requirements for Sustainability Statements, Delivery Plans, Health Impact Assessments, plus numerous site-specific technical studies (Transport, Ecology, Flood Risk, Contamination, Archaeology, Noise) add layers of complexity, cost, and time.

Discretionary Language: Phrases like 'where opportunities arise', 'appropriate', 'subject to viability', 'have regard to' appear throughout, reducing certainty for developers and decision-makers.

Contradictions & Trade-offs: Clear Priorities?

Effective planning requires balancing competing demands. The GNLP faces genuine environmental challenges like Nutrient Neutrality. However, it's crucial that other desirable goals like landscape or heritage protection are balanced proportionately against the pressing need for new homes, and not used inappropriately to block development.

Nutrient Neutrality: Acknowledged as a major constraint delaying permissions since March 2022 (Strategy, para 53). Policy 3 requires mitigation. This external requirement significantly impacts deliverability across much of the plan area.

Growth vs Protection Balance: The plan seeks high housing targets alongside strong protection policies for heritage (Policy 3), landscape (Policy 3), and biodiversity (Policy 3). The implementation of these policies will determine if the balance correctly reflects the scale of the housing need.

Absolute Constraints?: Nutrient Neutrality currently acts as a significant barrier. Designated heritage assets rightly receive substantial protection nationally.

Green Infrastructure Needs: Policy 3 requires GI provision, which is positive for biodiversity and amenity but reduces net developable area and can impact the viability of delivering other requirements like affordable housing.

Site Deliverability: Strategic Intent, Practical Challenges

The plan's focus on large strategic sites is logical for delivering volume. However, the monitoring framework itself highlights potential delivery risks (viability, infrastructure delays) even for these key locations. The extensive list of site-specific requirements adds further layers of complexity that could slow down delivery.

Strategic Site Focus: Most growth is planned on large STR sites (ENSRA, Anglia Square, Airport, NRP, Growth Triangle etc.).

Deliverability Risks Acknowledged: The Monitoring Framework (Table 2) includes triggers for review if strategic sites underperform on housing, affordable housing, or infrastructure delivery (e.g. STR.01, STR.02, STR.04, STR.07, STR.11).

Site-Specific Requirements: Individual site policies (Document 2) often detail numerous prerequisites, such as specific junction upgrades, school land provision, masterplan agreement, ecological mitigation, and various assessments, before development can commence.

Viability Considerations: Viability is noted as a factor, particularly for strategic sites (Monitoring triggers for STR.01, STR.02) and setting affordable housing levels (Policy 5), allowing for negotiation but potentially reducing expected contributions.

Action: "Assess why the housing targets are not being met. If there are viability concerns update the Viability Assessment. Consider whether a review of the plan should be undertaken..."

The plan's response to anticipated under-delivery leans towards review and reassessment rather than proactively simplifying requirements or removing identified barriers upfront.

Delivery Trajectory: Realistic Ramp-Up?

The housing trajectory projects meeting the 45k target by 2038. However, this relies on delivery rates increasing substantially from 2024/25 onwards, peaking significantly higher than recent completion rates, before tailing off. Given ongoing constraints, this projected acceleration appears highly optimistic.

Forecast vs Need: Total forecast supply (45,041) exceeds the LHN requirement (40,541). (Strategy, Appendix 4)

Delivery Profile: To meet the target, annual delivery needs to average ~2,250 homes. The trajectory shows a required peak delivery rate close to 3,000 homes per year in the late 2020s, significantly higher than the ~2,182/year average achieved 2018-2022. (Strategy, Appendix 4 Graph).

Reliance on Large Sites: The mid-to-late plan period delivery relies heavily on large strategic sites progressing without significant delays.

Buffer Adequacy: The 11% buffer provides some headroom, but prolonged issues (like Nutrient Neutrality or multiple large site viability problems) could consume this buffer, jeopardising even the lower LHN target.

Policy Language & Certainty: Clear Path or Obstacle Course?

Effective plans provide certainty. This plan's reliance on subjective terms like 'respect character', 'enhance setting', and 'where appropriate' creates ambiguity. This lack of clarity can empower obstruction and make it harder for developers, especially smaller ones, to bring forward compliant schemes efficiently.

Subjective Language: Widespread use of terms like "high-quality", "respect character", "enhance setting", "minimise impact", "where appropriate", "conserve significance" introduces subjectivity into decision-making. (Policies 2, 3, and site policies).

Conditional Policies: Development support is frequently conditional on further assessments (HIA, Transport, Ecology etc.) or meeting broadly defined criteria, reducing upfront clarity on requirements.

Decision-Maker Discretion: The combination of broad criteria (especially in Policies 2 and 3) and subjective wording gives planning officers and committees considerable scope for interpretation, which could hinder development even on allocated sites.
