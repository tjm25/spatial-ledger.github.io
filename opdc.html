Housing Target & Delivery: Ambitious Target... On Paper?

The Plan targets a minimum of 19,850 new homes over the 20-year period (2018-2038), averaging 993 homes per year (Policy SP4, H1). While this significantly exceeds the area's own calculated housing need (1,200 homes over 20 years), it aims to contribute to wider London need.

However, this target is below the indicative 25,500 homes potential identified in the Mayor's London Plan OAPF, suggesting a scaling back of ambition within the plan period itself (Para 3.3 vs SP4). Furthermore, the headline 50% affordable housing target (Policy SP4) comes with a crucial caveat: it's "subject to viability" (Policy H2, Para 3.21, Para 8.19). This creates significant uncertainty and risk that affordable delivery will fall short, especially given the high infrastructure costs.

Policy SP4, H1, H2; Paras 3.3, 3.21, 8.5-8.8, 8.17, 8.19.

Spatial Strategy: Right Focus, Wrong Scale?

The plan correctly focuses high-density, mixed-use development around the massive transport investment at Old Oak Common station (P1) and other accessible locations like North Acton (P7) and Scrubs Lane (P10). This embraces Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) principles, which is positive.

However, the sheer scale of land designated as protected Strategic Industrial Land (SIL) in Park Royal and Old Oak North (Policies SP5b, E1) severely restricts the land available for housing. While intensification of SIL is planned, it prioritises industrial/logistics jobs over homes, even potentially suitable brownfield sites near new transport links. Key complex sites like the Elizabeth Line and IEP depots are largely deferred beyond the plan period, representing missed opportunities for housing delivery sooner (Paras 3.86, 4.8, 4.15).

Policies P1, P2, P7, P9, P10, P11, SP5, SP6, E1; Figs 3.7, 9.4; Paras 3.86, 4.8, 4.15.

Hidden Constraints & Policy Blockers: Industrial Land & Discretion

The biggest blocker is the iron grip protection of Strategic Industrial Land (SIL) under Policy E1. While protecting *some* key logistics/industrial function is valid, the sheer scale of protected land severely limits housing potential in a prime, transport-rich location. Policy E1 allows *intensification* but resists housing release, arguably locking up land inefficiently.

Beyond SIL, standard planning policies on design (SP9, D3), heritage (D7), and amenity (D5) introduce significant discretion. Phrases like 'responds positively to context', 'enhances local character', or ensuring proposals don't cause 'unacceptable harm' (Policy D5) can be weaponised to delay or downscale housing schemes, despite the plan's headline support for high density.

Policy E1(b): Proposals must "achieve no net loss of industrial floorspace capacity and where feasible, intensify the use of sites..." - Prioritises floorspace over housing potential, even outside core industrial functions.
Policies E1, SP9, D3, D5, D7; Paras 9.9, 9.11.

Contradictions & Trade-offs: Housing vs Industry

The central contradiction is the plan's twin goals: delivering tens of thousands of homes *and* protecting/intensifying London's largest industrial estate. Policy DI1 ('Balancing Priorities') acknowledges trade-offs are needed, but the plan consistently prioritises industrial land retention (SIL) over housing potential outside of the core Old Oak station area.

The 50% affordable housing target (SP4) directly conflicts with the viability caveat (H2, DI1). Site Allocations: Complex & Conditional

The plan relies heavily on large, complex sites coming forward (Table 3.1). Many key sites around Old Oak Common station (P1) are dependent on HS2 construction timetables, public sector land release, significant infrastructure investment (acknowledged funding gaps - Para 11.8), and resolving operational rail constraints (depots).

While Site Allocations cover the 19,850 target (Table 3.1 shows ~18,900 minimum), the deliverability of these sites within the plan period is highly conditional and carries significant risk. The plan notes complexity repeatedly (Paras 3.80, 3.87, 4.7, 4.8).

Trajectory & Delivery: Backloaded and Hopeful

The Development Phasing map (Fig 3.16) clearly shows that the most significant housing delivery, particularly around the core Old Oak Common station area (P1), is backloaded to the later phases (6-10 years, 11-20 years, and even 21+ years). Early delivery (0-5 years) is concentrated on less complex sites on the periphery (North Acton, Scrubs Lane).

This backloading, combined with the reliance on complex sites and uncertain infrastructure funding, indicates a high risk of under-delivery against the plan's own targets within the 20-year period, let alone the higher OAPF potential. Delivery relies more on hope than certainty for the core regeneration area.

Figure 3.16; Policy SP10; Paras 3.81, 3.84, 3.85, 4.8.

Policy Language & Implementation Risk: Certainty Undermined

While the plan provides spatial certainty by designating areas for high density and tall buildings (Fig 3.15), this is undermined by discretionary policy language elsewhere. Policies related to design, character, heritage, and amenity (SP9, D3, D5, D7) use subjective terms like 'appropriate', 'sensitive', 'responds positively', giving significant leeway to decision-makers and objectors.

The biggest risk lies in the "subject to viability" caveat attached to the 50% affordable housing target (Policy H2, SP4). This signals that the target may not be achievable or enforced if developers argue it makes schemes unprofitable, potentially leading to much lower affordable housing delivery in practice.

Policy SP4(a)(ii): Supporting "an overarching 50% affordable housing target... subject to viability." - The key phrase that casts doubt on actual delivery.
Policies SP4, SP9, H2, D3, D5, D7, DI1; Paras 3.21, 8.17, 8.19.
