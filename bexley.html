Spatial Ledger Analysis: Bexley Local Plan

Adopted April 2023

Overall Verdict: Ambition Undermined by Constraints

While Bexley's Local Plan outlines a vision for sustainable growth, its effectiveness is likely hampered by adopting London-level housing targets rather than assessing local need, imposing restrictive borough-wide height limits, and embedding overly discretionary design policies that prioritise subjective 'character' protection, potentially hindering the delivery of much-needed homes.

Housing Target & Delivery: London Plan Fig Leaf?

Bexley's plan adopts the Greater London Authority's 10-year target of 6,850 homes (averaging 685 per year). Crucially, it explicitly states (para 1.15) that it is *not* required to conduct its own local Housing Needs Assessment. This allows the borough to avoid confronting the full scale of local housing need, particularly given the stark affordability crisis mentioned in the plan itself (para 2.4: average house price is ten times the average local wage).

While the plan's housing trajectory (Annex D) technically shows the 685/year target being met *on paper*, this relies heavily on future projections. Past delivery has often fallen short (Figure 2), and future success hinges on consistent delivery from allocated sites (many backloaded post-2027) and a very significant 45% contribution from inherently less certain small 'windfall' sites (para 1.40). This reliance on windfall appears optimistic.

Spatial Strategy: Right Focus, Deferred Potential

The plan correctly concentrates growth in 'Sustainable Development Locations' â€“ primarily town centres, areas near railway stations, and locations with good public transport access levels (PTALs 3-6), as shown in Policy SP1 and the Key Diagram (p.13). This aligns well with sustainable development principles.

However, the plan acknowledges the massive long-term potential of the Bexley Riverside Opportunity Area (estimated 23,000 homes by 2050 - para 1.18) but states its full potential "will not be realised in the plan period" (to 2038) due to dependence on unconfirmed infrastructure improvements (para 1.19). While some sites within the OA are allocated, the plan essentially defers unlocking the area's main contribution, underutilising strategically important regeneration land during the plan period.

Hidden Constraints: The 'Character' Defence & Strict Height Caps

Despite growth aspirations, the plan contains significant potential policy blockers. Design policies (SP5, DP11) repeatedly stress the need to "respect," "protect," or "enhance" existing character and amenity. This uses vague, subjective language that can be easily used to refuse development based on subjective interpretation rather than objective standards.

Most critically, Policy DP12 imposes very restrictive maximum building heights across the borough:

  • Only 15 metres (approx. 4 storeys) is the default maximum across most residential areas outside key zones (DP12.2c).
  • 25 metres (approx. 8 storeys) is the limit in most designated Sustainable Development Locations and Strategic Industrial Locations (DP12.2b).
  • 45 metres (approx. 15 storeys) is only deemed potentially acceptable in two small, specific 'Suitable Locations for Tall Buildings' near Abbey Wood and Belvedere stations (DP12.5, Figures 5 & 6).

This blanket 15m limit severely restricts opportunities for even modest density increases in many areas, forcing more pressure onto allocated sites or potentially the Green Belt. Any proposal daring to exceed 15m faces additional justification hurdles, including submitting alternative "human-scaled" designs (DP12.3).

"The proposed heights for buildings should reflect other design and policy requirements, including the requirement to have regard to the existing or emerging character and context of the area."

Policy DP12(1) - This framing prioritises subjective 'character' potentially over delivering needed homes at appropriate heights.

"Subject to part 1 above, the maximum height of buildings shall not normally be more than: [...] c. 15 metres across the rest of the borough."

Policy DP12(2c) - Codifies a restrictive low-rise default height limit, hindering intensification.

Contradictions & Trade-offs: Is 'Character' Code for 'No Change'?

There's a fundamental conflict between the plan's stated housing delivery goals (SP2, DP1) and the extensive protections afforded to various constraints â€“ Green Belt (SP8), heritage (SP6, DP14), local views (DP13), biodiversity (SP9, DP20), and especially the ill-defined concept of 'character' (SP5, DP11). While protection is necessary, the cumulative effect of these policies, combined with the strict height limits and discretionary language, creates a strong bias towards maintaining the status quo, potentially at the expense of housing delivery.

The 15m height limit appears a major, potentially unjustified, trade-off that significantly limits housing capacity in numerous suitable locations. Relying on London Plan targets instead of a local needs assessment avoids difficult conversations but doesn't solve the underlying affordability crisis facing Bexley residents.

Site Allocations & Trajectory: Deliverable Reality or Optimistic Forecast?

Part 2 of the plan allocates specific sites for development, focusing logically on brownfield land and underutilised areas within the defined Sustainable Development Locations (town centres, near stations). Major sites like ASDA/B&Q Belvedere (BEL01), Belvedere Gas Holders (BEL05), Erith Western Gateway (ERI01), Erith Riverside (ERI04), and the Crayford sites (CRA01-03) are included.

However, the housing trajectory (Annex D and Figure 2/3), while demonstrating *planned* delivery against the target, relies heavily on: (1) allocated sites delivering consistently from 2027/28 onwards, and (2) a very high (45%) and continuous contribution from small windfall sites (972 homes expected just in the first 5 years - Table 41). Given historical under-delivery and the plan's inherent policy constraints (height, character), this trajectory appears highly optimistic and vulnerable to delays or under-performance. The densities planned on some allocated sites may also be capped below their true potential by the plan's own height restrictions.

Policy Language & Implementation: A Recipe for Delay?

The frequent use of subjective terms like "appropriate," "respect," "enhance," "sympathetic," "complementary," and "unacceptable adverse impact" within key policies concerning design, character, heritage, and amenity (e.g., DP2, DP11, DP14, SP5, DP12) creates significant uncertainty for developers and invites subjective opposition during the planning application process. Instead of providing clear rules, this leaves considerable room for discretionary refusals, potentially slowing down, reducing, or blocking necessary housing projects, even on sites the plan itself has allocated for development. This introduces substantial implementation risk.
