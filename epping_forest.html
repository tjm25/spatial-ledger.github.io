Spatial Ledger Analysis: Epping Forest District Local Plan (2011-2033)

Adopted March 2023

Overall Verdict: Hindering Full Housing Delivery

This Local Plan sacrifices meeting identified housing need on the altar of Green Belt protection and environmental constraints, setting a target below requirements and relying heavily on complex, backloaded strategic sites and numerous small Green Belt releases fraught with policy hurdles.

Target vs Need: Constrained
Spatial Strategy: Dispersed Green Belt Release
Policy Blockers: High Risk
Deliverability: Optimistic & Backloaded

Housing Target & Delivery: Falling Short of Need

The Plan sets a housing target significantly lower than the objectively assessed need, explicitly citing Green Belt and environmental constraints as justification. While aiming for a step-change from past delivery, it bakes in under-provision against actual requirements from the start.

  • Annual Target: Minimum 11,400 homes 2011-2033 (Policy SP1), averaging 518 per year.
  • Target vs. OAN: Acknowledges an updated OAN share of ~12,573 homes (Para 2.47) but adopts the lower 11,400 figure based on an MoU distribution (Para 2.48, 2.59). This is a planned shortfall against identified need.
  • Compared to Past Delivery: Target (518/yr) is nearly double the historic average delivery rate (~275/yr 2011-2022), requiring a massive ramp-up (Appendix 5).
  • Constraints Lowering Target: Yes, explicitly. Green Belt (covering >90% of the District) and Epping Forest SAC impacts are cited as reasons for not meeting full OAN (Para 2.66, 2.146).
  • Land Allocation: The Plan allocates sites aiming for 12,199 homes (Appendix 5), exceeding the minimum 11,400 target *on paper*. However, deliverability is questionable (see later sections).

Spatial Strategy: Green Belt Erosion by a Thousand Cuts

While claiming to prioritise sustainable locations, the strategy relies heavily on releasing numerous Green Belt sites, often small parcels on the edge of villages. Large strategic sites near Harlow dominate the numbers but face complex delivery challenges, while core towns see relatively modest growth.

  • Prioritised Locations: Large Garden Communities around Harlow (SP4), South Epping (MP01), North Weald Bassett (MP02/03). Some intensification within settlements like Epping and Loughton is planned (Para 5.12, 5.30).
  • Urban/Transport Hubs: Some sites utilise transport links (e.g., station car parks), but the scale is limited compared to the reliance on edge-of-settlement Green Belt releases.
  • Rural/Village Sites: Numerous small Green Belt sites allocated across many villages (e.g., Ongar, Roydon, Nazeing, Thornwood, Sheering, Stapleford Abbotts â€“ see Policies P8-P15 & Part 2). This disperses development rather than concentrating it sustainably.
  • Underutilised Areas: North Weald Airfield (MP03/NWB.E4A) is a huge site primarily allocated for employment/aviation, with limited housing relative to its potential scale.
Quote: "The Council has considered the spatial options... [including] Expansion of the settlement..." (e.g. Para 5.12, 5.30, 5.60, 5.111, 5.131, 5.151). Frequent justification for Green Belt release around multiple settlements.

Hidden Constraints & Policy Blockers: Death by Discretion

The Plan is loaded with development management policies using vague, discretionary language concerning design, character, heritage, landscape, and amenity. These provide ample opportunity for obstruction and downscaling, undermining certainty even on allocated sites.

  • Restrictive Policies: DM3 (Landscape/Ancient Landscapes), DM7 (Historic Environment), DM9 (High Quality Design), SP2 (Place Shaping), DM1 (Habitat), DM2 (SAC/SPA), DM5 (Green/Blue Infrastructure). Part 2 site requirements add site-specific layers (e.g., trees, views, noise, archaeology).
  • Density/Height Caps: Policy SP2 B offers density guidance but includes a loophole for lower densities if sites are 'particularly sensitive'. Densities on some allocated Green Belt sites are very low (e.g., STAP.R1 at 15 dph).
  • Discretionary Wording: Frequent use of terms like "relate positively," "sensitive to," "minimise harm," "where appropriate," "have regard to" (Para 4.66, DM9, DM3, DM7 etc.) invites subjective interpretation and challenge.
  • Implementation Hurdles: Requirement for Strategic Masterplans (SP4, Site Policies) and Concept Framework Plans (Para 2.105) adds significant process steps before planning permission, increasing risk and delay.

Contradictions & Trade-offs: Green Belt Wins, Housing Loses

The Plan explicitly states the need to release Green Belt due to unmet housing need but simultaneously claims to protect its general extent. Protection of the Epping Forest SAC is also paramount, leading to complex mitigation strategies that add cost and uncertainty.

  • Goals Blocking Housing: Green Belt protection is the primary justification for not meeting the full OAN (Para 2.146). Epping Forest SAC impacts (air quality, recreation) are major constraints requiring mitigation (DM2, Para 4.15-4.22).
  • Conflicting Goals: Policy SP5 claims to protect the Green Belt's general extent while Policies SP1/SP4 and Chapter 5 allocate numerous sites within it. Delivering 11,400+ homes while mitigating SAC impacts presents inherent tensions.
  • Transparency: The Plan *acknowledges* the need for Green Belt release is driven by housing need unmet elsewhere (Para 2.146) and discusses SAC constraints openly. The site selection methodology is outlined (Para 2.70).
Quote: "National planning policy requires that exceptional circumstances are demonstrated to justify any alteration to the Green Belt boundary... The justification for altering the Green Belt boundaries in this Plan arose from local circumstances: the extent of the Green Belt and subsequent deficiency of land within the existing settlements... and the inability of neighbouring authorities to meet the needs of the District." (Para 2.141-2.142). Acknowledges need forces Green Belt release.

Site Viability & Density: Constraints Cloud Delivery

While specific sites are allocated, many come with a long list of constraints and requirements detailed in Part 2, raising questions about their actual deliverability and viability. Densities vary wildly, with some Green Belt releases planned at questionably low levels.

  • Deliverability Concerns: Many Part 2 site entries list multiple constraints: heritage settings, TPOs, flood risk zones (even if sequentially preferred), noise, air quality, access upgrades needed, complex masterplanning required (e.g. EPP.R1/R2, WAL.R1/R2/R3/T1, ONG.R1/R2).
  • Prioritisation: Heavy reliance on large, complex Garden Communities and strategic sites (SP4, South Epping, North Weald) which take longer to deliver.
  • Site Densities: Variable. Some town centre sites are high density (e.g. EPP.R6 @ 100dph), but edge-of-settlement/Green Belt sites range from moderate (ONG.R4 @ 38dph) to very low (STAP.R1 @ 15dph). Low densities on Green Belt releases seem inefficient given the stated pressures.

Trajectory & Delivery: Ambitious Ramp-Up, High Risk

The Plan relies on a massively optimistic, backloaded housing trajectory, requiring delivery rates far exceeding historic performance, particularly in the later years. This strategy carries a high risk of under-delivery even against the constrained target.

  • Historic vs Planned: Plan requires average 518 homes/year, nearly double the ~275/year achieved 2011-2022 (Appendix 5).
  • Backloading: Delivery is heavily skewed to later years: 2,500 homes planned 2022-27 (500/yr) rising to 5,880 homes 2027-33 (980/yr). This relies heavily on strategic sites delivering at scale.
  • Unrealistic Assumptions?: Achieving nearly 1,000 homes per year in the late 2020s/early 2030s seems highly ambitious given constraints, infrastructure needs, and market cycles.
  • Planned Shortfall vs Need: The trajectory aims to exceed the 11,400 target (showing 12,199), providing some buffer. However, this is still a shortfall against the OAN share (~12,573).
