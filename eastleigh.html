Spatial Ledger Verdict: Eastleigh Local Plan (2016-2036)

This Local Plan was knowingly adopted with a significant housing shortfall, kicking the can down the road by relying on past permissions and mandating an immediate review.
Target vs Need: Fails
Spatial Strategy: Constrained
Policy Blockers: High Risk
Contradiction Score: Severe

Housing Target: Falling Short from Day One

Eastleigh acknowledges its housing need but adopts a plan that explicitly fails to meet it. While setting a target aligned with objective need, the numbers simply don't add up, creating a deficit from the moment of adoption.

The Plan aims for 14,580 homes (729 per year) between 2016-2036 (Policy S2), reflecting the identified need. However, the Plan itself admits this target won't be met. The identified supply totals only 11,966 homes, leaving a shortfall of 2,614 dwellings (para 4.11). This failure is blamed on the removal of a proposed Strategic Growth Option (SGO) during the examination process. Adopting a plan known to be insufficient is a significant failure in planning responsibility.

Key Numbers (Policy S2 & Trajectory p33):
  • Target (2016-2036): 14,580
  • Completions (Apr 16 - Mar 19): 2,572
  • Commitments (Permissions/Resolutions Apr 19): 7,187 + 513 = 7,700
  • Windfall Allowance (Apr 19 - Mar 36): 1,475 (approx 87/year)
  • Allocated in this Plan: Just 732
  • Total Identified Supply: 11,966
  • RESULT: Shortfall of 2,614 homes
Direct Admission of Failure: "The pattern of delivery set out in Strategic Policy S2 results in a shortfall of 2,614 dwellings against the target of 14,580... This is primarily due to the deletion, as recommended by the Local Plan Examination Inspector, of the Strategic Growth Option... The Inspector recommended that this shortfall be addressed in an early review of the local plan." (para 4.11, p31).

Where Will Homes Go?: Past Permissions & Tight Constraints

The plan leans heavily on large greenfield sites that already had planning permission before this plan was finalised. New allocations are minimal and scattered. Significant areas are effectively sterilised by protective policies like Settlement Gaps.

Major delivery relies on sites like Stoneham Park, Horton Heath, and Boorley Green (Policy S3a), which secured consent under previous processes. The mere 732 newly allocated homes (Policy S3c) are spread across smaller urban and edge-of-settlement sites (detailed in Chapter 6). While brownfield is mentioned first (para 3.6), the actual delivery strategy doesn't reflect this prioritisation for *new* supply. Crucially, Settlement Gap policies (S6) and restrictive Countryside policies (S5) severely limit where new housing can even be considered, protecting separation often at the expense of meeting needs near existing infrastructure. The Hamble peninsula is explicitly excluded from significant growth (para 3.7(l)).

Key Spatial Constraints:
  • Policy S5: Restricts development in the countryside to very specific uses.
  • Policy S6: Protects defined gaps between settlements (see Fig 2, p42), severely limiting development potential in these areas.
  • Policy S7: Protects the coast.

Hidden Blockers & Policy Hurdles

Beyond the obvious shortfall, a web of policies creates further risk. Restrictive criteria on design, character, density, and environmental factors provide ample opportunity to delay or refuse much-needed housing, even on allocated sites.

Policies like DM1 (General Criteria) demand development is 'compatible with' and 'respects' local character, terms which are subjective and can be used to block denser, more efficient development. While DM23 sets a minimum density of 40 dwellings per hectare, it includes caveats allowing lower densities if 'justified', potentially weakening the standard. Numerous policies require detailed assessments (Habitats Regulations Assessment, Flood Risk, Transport, Heritage, Ecology - e.g. DM5, DM11, DM12, DM13) adding cost, time, and uncertainty. Settlement Gaps (S6) act as a major, explicit blocker, functioning much like Green Belt.

Potential Blockers:
  • Subjective design/character tests (DM1).
  • Weakened density targets (DM23).
  • Strict protection of Settlement Gaps (S6) and Countryside (S5).
  • Extensive buffer requirements for nature sites (DM11).
  • Complex assessment requirements (HRA, FRA, TRA etc.).

Contradictions & Trade-Offs: Papering Over the Cracks

The Plan's central contradiction is admitting its housing target failure while still being adopted. It acknowledges the need for an immediate review, effectively confirming the adopted document is inadequate from the start. Environmental and landscape protections appear prioritised over meeting housing needs within the plan period.

The Council claims a 'proactive and sustainable approach' (Foreword, p3) while adopting a plan with a known 2,614 home deficit. Objectives for housing delivery (Objective x) clash with the reality of the allocated supply (S2/S3) and restrictive spatial policies (S5/S6). The reliance on an "early review" (para 4.12) is an admission that difficult decisions about housing location have been deferred, not resolved.

Kicking the Can: "This shortfall... should be addressed through an early review of the local plan. Accordingly, the Council will commence an update of this Local Plan within 1 year of its adoption." (para 4.12, p32) - Why adopt a failing plan only to immediately review it?

Site Allocations & Delivery: A Plan Reliant on the Past

The vast majority of housing delivery anticipated in this Plan (over 90% of the supply identified excluding completions) comes from sites that ALREADY had planning permission or a resolution to grant before this plan was adopted. The Plan allocates precious few new sites itself.

Policies S2 and S3 make this clear: 7,700 homes are from existing commitments vs only 732 homes from new allocations in Chapter 6. These new allocations (e.g. FO1-3, BU1-5, CF1-4, AL1-2, HE2-6, BO1-4, WE1-3) are mostly small to medium scale and insufficient to address the overall need or the identified shortfall. Their deliverability depends on navigating the various Development Management policies.

Policy Language & Implementation Risk

While some strategic policies are clear (like the housing numbers), many Development Management (DM) policies rely on discretionary language. Terms like 'unacceptable impact', 'appropriate', 'sympathetic', 'significant adverse effect' lack precision and give considerable leeway to decision-makers, potentially creating inconsistency and risk for applicants proposing needed development.

The requirement for numerous, potentially complex, assessments (HRA, FRA, TRA, Noise, Air Quality, Heritage Statements etc.) across many policies adds layers of process that can slow down or deter development, particularly on smaller sites. While necessary in principle, their cumulative burden and potential for subjective interpretation needs careful monitoring to ensure they don't become de facto blockers.
