Spatial Ledger Analysis: B&NES Local Plan Partial Update (Adopted Jan 2023)

VERDICT: This 'update' doubles down on delay, layering complex new green rules onto an outdated strategy without tackling the fundamental need for more homes.

Target vs Need: POOR
Spatial Strategy: STALLED
Policy Blockers: HIGH RISK
Contradictions: SIGNIFICANT

Housing Target: Stuck in the Past

This isn't a real update – it's tinkering. The LPPU deliberately avoids reviewing the core housing target set years ago (around 13,000 homes by 2029), before the housing crisis deepened and before B&NES declared climate/ecological emergencies. While it adds some new sites (Policy DW1, Table 1C shows 940 new allocations), the document states this is primarily to 'replenish housing supply' to meet the existing target, not increase ambition.

By refusing to update the target or the overall spatial strategy, the LPPU locks in likely under-delivery relative to actual housing need. It acknowledges a full new Local Plan (2022-2042) is underway (p.3, para 5d), effectively admitting this 'update' is a stop-gap measure that fails to address the scale of the challenge.

Evidence: LPPU Introduction (p.3, para 5b) explicitly states the scope was confined 'without significantly changing the strategic policy framework... i.e. the spatial priorities; the spatial strategy; or the strategic housing and job growth requirements'. The housing target (~13,000 homes) dates back to the original Core Strategy/Placemaking Plan (pre-2017). Policy DW1 (p.6) outlines delivery against this old target.

Spatial Strategy: No Meaningful Change

Where should homes go? This update doesn't ask the hard questions. It explicitly avoids changing the 'spatial strategy' (p.3, para 5b). While some Green Belt land is released/reallocated (East Keynsham KE3c/KE3d, Park & Ride sites SB26), this is done within the constraints of the old plan, not as part of a fresh assessment of where sustainable development should occur.

The refusal to revisit the spatial strategy means opportunities in accessible locations might be missed, while pressure could remain on less suitable areas. The justification seems to be the pending full Local Plan, but this delays necessary strategic decisions.

Evidence: Para 5b (p.3) confirms no change to spatial strategy. Green Belt changes mentioned (p.5 para 50 for E. Keynsham; p.8 policy DW1(4) & p.111 policy SB26 for P&R sites). East Keynsham allocations KE3c (p.210) & KE3d (p.215) replace previously safeguarded land.

Contradiction Alert: The LPPU states (p.3, para 5b) its scope was confined 'without significantly changing the strategic... housing... requirements'. How can this be reconciled with addressing urgent climate/ecological emergencies declared after those requirements were set?

Policy Blockers: Layering on Constraints

This LPPU significantly increases the policy burden on new development. While environmental goals are important, adding demanding new standards without a full plan review risks stifling delivery. Key blockers include:

  • Zero Carbon & Energy Metrics (SCR6): Requires very high energy performance (Space Heating <30kWh/m², Total Energy <40kWh/m²) and matching renewables, or costly off-site financial contributions (p.31).
  • Embodied Carbon (SCR8): Requires assessment and demanding targets (<900kgCO₂e/m²) for large sites, adding complexity and cost (p.32).
  • Biodiversity Net Gain (NE3a): Mandates a minimum 10% BNG using DEFRA metrics, secured for 30 years. While legally required soon, its imposition here adds another hurdle without strategic context (p.44).
  • Irreplaceable Habitats (NE3): Development 'will not be permitted' unless 'wholly exceptional circumstances' exist (p.41). This is a very high bar.
  • Landscape & Heritage: Existing strong protections remain (e.g., HE1, NE2 'Great weight' for AONB), with the new burdens layered on top.
  • Accessibility (H7): Specific % requirements for M4(2)/M4(3) standards add further build costs (p.66).

The cumulative impact of these policies, added in a partial update without assessing overall viability impacts on the (old) housing target, creates significant risk of slowing down or stopping needed development.

Contradictions: Green Goals vs. Housing Reality?

The LPPU presents a fundamental contradiction. It champions the Climate and Ecological Emergency declarations (p.3, 11) and introduces ambitious green policies (SCR6, SCR8, NE3a). However, it simultaneously refuses to update the housing target or spatial strategy (p.3) to reflect these new realities or assess the trade-offs.

Can B&NES realistically deliver even its outdated housing target while imposing these new standards, especially on limited, constrained land supply? The LPPU doesn't provide the evidence. It risks using laudable environmental aims as a shield for inaction on housing supply, making development harder and more expensive without a clear plan for how the numbers will still be met.

Evidence: Climate Emergency declaration referenced p.3, p.11. New sustainability policies SCR6-9, NE3a detailed pp.30-37, 44. Explicit statement of not changing strategic housing targets/strategy p.3 para 5b.

Performative Policy? Para 23b (p.4) states "new homes and development need to be zero carbon or net positive carbon from now”. Yet the LPPU fails to provide the updated strategic plan or viability evidence to show how this can be delivered alongside sufficient housing numbers.

Site Allocations: Incremental Changes, Increased Hurdles

The LPPU allocates several new sites, mainly repurposing safeguarded land (East Keynsham KE3c/d), adding small village sites (Treetops KE5, Silver St SSV21), and confirming new uses (Locksbrook Hub SB22, Weston Island SB23, St Martins SB25, Sion Hill SB24). Park & Ride sites (SB26) are removed from Green Belt for 'Interchange Hub' uses.

While some allocations are positive (e.g., utilising safeguarded land), the total number of homes added seems modest (~1000-1200 across all new allocations) relative to the likely need. Crucially, all sites now face the tougher SCR and NE3a policy requirements, potentially impacting their deliverability and pace without updated viability testing published as part of this LPPU.

Evidence: New allocations listed in Vol 6 Appendix 1 (Table 11 implies K2, NR2, V8, V3 deleted/replaced). New policies SB22-26, KE3c/d, KE5, SSV21 introduced. Policy DW1 Table 1C (p.6) claims 940 new homes via LPPU. Specific site policies (e.g. KE3c p.210) detail numbers.

Delivery & Certainty: More Risk, Less Clarity

By adding complex new policies (SCR6, SCR8, NE3a, updated NE3, H7) in a partial update, the LPPU increases uncertainty for developers and decision-makers. The plan relies on an old trajectory ending in 2029. The cumulative burden of policy requirements risks slowing delivery and making the planning process more contentious.

The frequent references to needing further detailed assessments (Transport Assessments, Heritage Impact Assessments, LVIAs, ecological surveys, BNG calculations, Embodied Carbon Assessments) at the application stage, while standard practice, highlight the significant hurdles developers must overcome, potentially deterring investment or leading to delays.

Evidence: Plan period ends 2029 (p.3). Multiple new assessment requirements introduced (SCR6-8, NE3a, site policies). Lack of LPPU-specific viability assessment covering cumulative policy impact.
